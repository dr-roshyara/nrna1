# Understanding Contribution Metrics

A guide for organization leaders to interpret contribution data and make decisions.

---

## Key Metrics Explained

### Metric 1: Total Points

**What it is:** Sum of all contributions in your organization

**Example:**
```
April 2026 Totals:
- 22 members logged contributions
- 1,750 total points awarded
- Average: 80 points per person
```

**What it means:**
- **Growing total** = More engagement, or more high-value work
- **Stable total** = Consistent volunteers
- **Declining total** = Retention problem, or fewer major projects

**Action:**
- If declining: Launch re-engagement campaign
- If growing: Celebrate and sustain momentum
- If stable: Maintain, but look for growth opportunities

---

### Metric 2: Number of Contributors

**What it is:** How many people logged at least one contribution

**Example:**
```
Contributors by Month:
- January: 15 members
- February: 18 members
- March: 21 members
- April: 22 members

Trend: +7 new contributors in 4 months (+47%)
```

**What it means:**
- **Increasing** = You're attracting new volunteers ✓
- **Flat** = Contributor base is stable
- **Decreasing** = People are leaving or not volunteering

**Action:**
- If increasing: Excellent! You're growing. Onboard them well.
- If flat: Consider new outreach campaigns
- If decreasing: Something's wrong. Survey members about barriers.

---

### Metric 3: Average Points per Contributor

**What it is:** Total points ÷ Number of contributors

**Example:**
```
April: 1,750 points ÷ 22 members = 80 points average per person

Comparison:
- If average was 40 (April 2025): You're getting 2× more value!
- If average was 100 (April 2024): Effort per person has declined
```

**What it means:**
- **High average** (100+) = People doing substantial work
- **Medium average** (50–100) = Mix of micro and standard contributions
- **Low average** (0–50) = Mostly micro contributions, or many non-engaged members

**Action:**
- If high and rising: Great engagement. Sustain.
- If high but declining: Less work being done. Investigate why.
- If low: Normal for new programs. Focus on recruiting and onboarding.

---

### Metric 4: Distribution Across Tracks

**What it is:** Breakdown of contributions by track (Micro / Standard / Major)

**Example:**
```
April Contributions:
- Micro (≤30 pts): 12 contributions, 150 points (9%)
- Standard (31-200 pts): 8 contributions, 800 points (46%)
- Major (201+ pts): 2 contributions, 800 points (45%)

Balance: Good mix. Not too reliant on any one track.
```

**What it means:**

**Healthy distribution:**
- 50–60% Standard (regular volunteers doing organized work)
- 30–40% Micro (frequent small help)
- 10–20% Major (big projects, partnerships)

**Red flag distributions:**
- 90% Micro, 10% Standard (everyone doing small tasks, no big projects)
- 100% Major, 0% others (only big projects, no regular volunteers)

**Action:**
- If too much Micro: Encourage people to organize larger projects
- If too much Major: Build base of regular Standard contributors
- If good balance: Maintain. Your contribution culture is healthy.

---

### Metric 5: Skills Distribution

**What it is:** Which skills are most represented in contributions?

**Example:**
```
Skills in April:
- Teaching/Mentoring: 35% of points ✓
- Event Planning: 25% of points ✓
- Engineering: 15% of points ✓
- Healthcare: 12% of points ✓
- Legal/Admin: 8% of points ✗
- Other: 5%

Gap: Legal and admin skills underutilized.
```

**What it means:**
- **High representation** = That skill is valued, or you have people
- **Low representation** = Skill is rare, or not being utilized
- **Missing entirely** = You need those skills

**Action:**
- If legal/healthcare low: Recruit professionals in those fields
- If specific skill absent: Advertise for people with that skill
- If all balanced: Good diversity. Keep it up.

---

### Metric 6: New Contributor Retention

**What it is:** Of people who contributed in Month 1, what % contributed in Month 2?

**Example:**
```
January: 15 contributors
February: 18 contributors (15 returning + 3 new)
Retention: 15÷15 = 100%

March: 21 contributors (17 returning + 4 new)
Retention: 17÷18 = 94%

April: 22 contributors (18 returning + 4 new)
Retention: 18÷21 = 86%
```

**What it means:**
- **High retention (80%+)** = People stay engaged ✓
- **Medium retention (50–80%)** = Some dropoff, but acceptable
- **Low retention (<50%)** = People are leaving. Investigate.

**Action:**
- If high: Sustain what you're doing
- If declining: Survey exiting volunteers. Why did they stop?
- If low: Improve onboarding or reduce barriers

---

### Metric 7: Top Contributor Concentration

**What it is:** Do a few people do most of the work, or is it distributed?

**Example:**
```
April Points Distribution:
- Top 1 person: 1,250 points (71%)
- Top 3 people: 3,080 points (176%)

Wait, that's more than 100%? Let me recalculate...

Actually: Top 3 = 68% of total points
Top 5 = 82% of total points
Remaining 17 = 18% of total points

Concentration: Very high. Reliant on few people.
```

**What it means:**
- **Distributed (top 3 = 30–40% of points)** = Healthy. Many contributors doing substantive work.
- **Concentrated (top 3 = 50–70%)** = You have core volunteers, but not much depth.
- **Highly concentrated (top 3 = 80%+)** = Risk. Few people carrying org.

**Action:**
- If distributed: Excellent. Build on this.
- If concentrated: Succession planning. Who replaces Dr. Sharma if she burns out?
- If highly concentrated: Crisis mode. Recruit and develop backup leaders.

---

### Metric 8: Contribution Quality (Average Points per Contribution)

**What it is:** Total points ÷ Number of contributions

**Example:**
```
April:
- 22 contributions logged
- 1,750 points awarded
- Average: 80 points per contribution

Interpretation:
- High-quality contributions (lots of evidence, synergy, sustainability)
- Mix of tracks and proof types
```

**What it means:**
- **High average (80+)** = Well-verified, substantial work
- **Medium average (40–80)** = Mix of quality levels
- **Low average (<40)** = Mostly micro contributions, or weak evidence

**Action:**
- If quality dropping: Educate contributors on proof types, synergy, sustainability
- If quality stable: Good. Keep verifying fairly.
- If quality rising: Contributions are getting stronger. Why? Learn and replicate.

## Making Decisions from Metrics

### Use Case 1: "Should I recruit more?"

**Data to check:**
```
Metric: Number of contributors trending
January: 10
February: 10
March: 11
April: 11

Trend: Flat. No growth in 3 months.

Decision: Yes, recruit. You're stable but not expanding.
Action: Launch recruitment campaign for 5 new volunteers.
```

---

### Use Case 2: "Are we sustainable?"

**Data to check:**
```
Metric: Top contributor concentration
Top 3: Dr. Sharma (1,250), Mrs. Patel (980), Raj (700)
Top 3 total: 2,930 / 4,200 = 70%

Decision: Risky. Not sustainable.
Action: Develop 2–3 backup leaders. Distribute work. Offer leadership training.
```

---

### Use Case 3: "Where should we focus recruitment?"

**Data to check:**
```
Metric: Skills distribution
Legal: 2% of points (only 1 person)
Healthcare: 15% of points (but need more)
Engineering: 8% of points (could be higher)

Decision: Recruit legal professionals (strategic need) and healthcare workers (underutilized).
Action: Contact law firms, hospitals. Highlight how their skills are valued.
```

---

### Use Case 4: "Why are people leaving?"

**Data to check:**
```
Metric: Retention declining
February retention: 90%
March retention: 75%
April retention: 60%

Decision: Something's wrong. Investigate.
Action: Survey who left. Common reasons?
- Too hard to log contributions?
- Admin feedback negative?
- Better opportunity elsewhere?
- Burnout?

Fix: Address the root cause.
```

## Questions?

- **"How often should I review metrics?"** — Monthly is good. Quarterly for strategic review.
- **"What if metrics look bad?"** — Don't panic. Investigate. Is it seasonal? Permanent? Fixable?
- **"Should I share metrics with members?"** — Yes! Transparency builds trust and motivation.
- **"What's a good benchmark?"** — Growing total points, stable-to-growing contributors, 70–30 distribution.
